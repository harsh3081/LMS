# ADR-009: Two-Layer Observability — a First-Class PostgreSQL Audit Log Plus Standard Application Logging and APM

## Status

Accepted — 2026-07-15

## Context

LMS has two distinct kinds of "what happened" tracking that are easy to conflate but serve different purposes. First, a business/compliance audit trail is required by the BRD to drive the reassignment-history requirement — TL and SM/GM roles must be able to query who reassigned which lead/enquiry, when, and (implicitly) why — which is a domain-level, queryable, permanent record. Second, standard operational observability is needed to understand system health, diagnose errors, and monitor uptime against the 99.5% business-hours SLA (ADR-005) — a concern about the running system, not about business events. Treating both needs as a single "logging" concern risks either under-serving the compliance query requirement (log files are not easily queryable by business roles) or over-building operational tooling (a full ELK/Grafana stack) that is disproportionate to dealership scale.

## Decision

We will implement two distinct, separately-designed layers. The business/compliance audit trail will be a first-class, immutable `audit_log` table in PostgreSQL (ADR-002), row-level scoped by `location_id`/`dealer_group_id` (ADR-003), recording who performed what action on which entity and when — reassignments in particular — and exposed to TL/SM/GM roles through the application's own query/reporting endpoints rather than through log tooling. Operational observability will be handled separately via structured JSON application logging (emitted by the NestJS backend) plus a basic Application Performance Monitoring (APM) and uptime-probe setup appropriate to dealership scale, rather than a full log-aggregation/dashboarding stack such as ELK or Grafana.

## Consequences

**Positive**: Putting the compliance audit trail in the transactional database means it inherits PostgreSQL's durability, transactional consistency, and query capability, so TL/SM/GM reassignment-history queries are simple, fast, indexed SQL queries rather than log-mining; keeping operational logging separate and modest in scope (structured JSON logs plus basic APM/uptime checks) avoids the cost and operational burden of a full observability stack that dealership-scale traffic does not justify.

**Negative**: The `audit_log` table will grow monotonically over time and needs a retention/archival strategy to avoid unbounded growth in the primary database; "basic" APM and uptime probing gives less deep diagnostic visibility (e.g. distributed tracing, detailed performance flame graphs) than a full observability stack would, which may need to be revisited if the system's operational complexity grows significantly (e.g. after Booking/Invoicing/Payments integration).

**Neutral**: The `audit_log` schema (actor, action, entity, before/after state, timestamp, location scope) must be defined as part of technical design and treated as append-only/immutable at the application layer; the specific APM/uptime-probe tooling is an implementation-level choice to be finalized during technical design and does not change this ADR's architectural commitment to keeping compliance audit and operational logging as two distinct layers.
